Prologue

Start

 

 

            The proliferation of cookbooks, books on nutrition, magazines and other publications devoted to these subjects and, therefore, the number of people interested in them, is one of the most notable phenomena of our time. This need or sense of curiosity is clearly a result of the progress which has been made with respect to health matters in today's society, not only on an individual level but for society as a whole. As far as Spanish cooking is concerned, which has never been given much notoriety throughout its history, there is also a sense of evening the score with French and Italian cooking, which has always overshadowed that of its European counterpart.

 

            This lack of interest in Spanish cooking is quite odd considering how good Spanish cooking has always been. There has always been a wealth of tasty wild game, fruits and vegetables in Spain for which the Italians and especially the French owe a lot. Spain is where the seed and the original name came from for apricots, artichokes, aubergines (eggplant), pumpkin, asparagus... these products being the raw materials for many foods and dishes such as meatballs, marinades, stews, casseroles, jams and a variety of sweets and pastries. And let's not forget the great American contribution to the European diet, made possible by the Spanish, which solved the nutritional problem of the entire continent.

 

            In fact, Spain had everything, but not for everyone. The poor, which made up the vast majority of the population, had to make do with very little, and even that very little was hard to come by, and this is reflected in many Spanish proverbs. The poor were forced to follow the dictum of the poor nobleman under whom Lazarillo de Tormes served: "No hay tal cosa en el mundo, para vivir mucho, como comer poco" which could be loosely translated as "Eat little to live long". Travellers to Spain, especially the French, were shocked to discover how poor the taverns and innswere, and like Furetière, came to the conclusion, that "the Spanish live on garlic". Upon returning, they would tell of famished noblemen who strolled around with toothpicks between their teeth, in order to give the appearance that they had just eaten. The issue was a matter of quantity, not quality.

 

            To eat well then meant to eat a lot, whether it was meat, other dishes or good quality fruit, without too much concern for how the food was prepared. Foods that required more extensive preparation did not usually satisfy everyone. This is the case of blood pudding, which Juan de Arguijo speaks about: "One person, a real enemy of blood pudding, said that to eat it, it has to be roasted over a fire, garnished with orange, put between two plates, placed on the window sill and given a good kick into the street." Nevertheless, the poor considered blood pudding to be a food fit for a king and was therefore constantly mentioned in literature of the Golden Age.

 

            In the Canary Islands the problem of nutrition was an even more difficult one to resolve. This depended largely on the success of local agriculture, and droughts. And bad wheat harvests often ravaged the population, especially on the poorer islands. On several occasions Lanzarote and Fuerteventura were almost deserted due to the exodus of their people, who emigrated to the larger islands, especially Tenerife, just for the sake of survival. During the drought of 1785, the people of the island of El Hierro lived on plants "which had never been considered fit for human consumption, and many not even for animals"; on La Gomera the people were forced to eat ferns. People on the larger islands lived somewhat better because they were able to buy wheat from abroad. On the other hand, as was the case of the Spanish mainland, a person's diet often just depended on his economic situation. Although we do not have much information about the sweets and delicacies that the rich ate, we do know they consumed certain kinds of sweets made from honey and sugar, some of which are still made today. They ate figs, dates and bananas, the latter being sent to friends and relatives on the Spanish mainland in the form of preserves, tightly packed in jars. They would turn almost black and probably sour before arriving, and I assume no one would want to eat them today. However, in those days, these preserves were a much appreciated gift in Madrid.

 

            In any case, the islands as a whole, and particularly the larger islands, offered an abundant and varied diet. It is worth mentioning again however that the distribution of food left much to be desired. When they did eat, the poor islanders ate little and poorly. Due to a lack of wood and the money to buy it, most of the islanders never ate hot food until well into the 19th century. Some ate too much and others poorly. The composition of their diet was aberrant (and in some ways still is), consisting of about 90% cereals (principally bread and gofio - toasted grains) (as opposed to 65% on the Spanish mainland, 50% in France, 42% in England), and about 7% milk and meat (29% on the Spanish mainland, 46% in France, 53% in England), according to a study done in 1962. Meat also lost a large part of its dietary importance, for the daily consumption of meat in Santa Cruz dropped from 45 grams per person in  1860 to 17 grams in 1897, when the national average was 38 grams per person.

 

            These characteristics of the local diet, which have changed greatly in the past 40 years, together with the difficulty in preserving food, the bad quality of potable water and the preference Canarians have for fish (in most cases salted), have been the cause in the past of many digestive problems and made it difficult for a Canarian cuisine to develop, something which could not be conceived of in those times, but which now is a reality. There is no doubt that in earlier times one could eat well on the islands, but eating well back then was understood in a different way than today. Quantity was the criteria. For example, in Santa Cruz de Tenerife the Restaurante del Inglés had the best reputation, for in 1864 a typical lunch would consist of three main courses: eggs, fish and meat, plus fruit, wine and coffee. Nowadays, when we speak of good eating, we think exclusively of the quality and merit of the cooking.

 

            Nevertheless, since the past century, or even before, a peculiar form of Canarian cuisine has existed, which has been based both on quality and variety, and which has gained little attention because cooking was considered to be the task and concern of housewives and, has been, in a certain sense, an art which has never really reached the masses. Young women of good families learned the art of cooking from their mothers or grandmothers, and they did not disdain the kitchen which, for better or for worse, was their absolute domain. They learned, experimented and invented; they would pass on their latest recipes to friends and relatives, and they frequently kept a record of them in order not to forget them. These activities, which were a source of amusement for them, have taken on an importance that they certainly would never have imagined. We must not only thank them for giving us a view of how people used to eat and live 100 years ago, but also for something we think about less: they laid the foundation of today's Canarian cuisine and awakened everyone's interest in a sensible diet, based on quality and variety. They would feel proud to know that, in a sense, their work has served to contribute to the betterment of their fellow men.

 

 

ALEJANDRO CIORANESCU

PROF. OF HISTORY (*)
