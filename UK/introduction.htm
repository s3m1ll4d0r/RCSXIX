            Although cooking is one of the oldest sciences in the world, it has remained in the dominion of oral tradition until well into the 20th century. And despite the fact that cooking has been one of the main protagonists of history, it has been for the most part forgotten by historians.

 

            It is surprising to observe the lack of literature on the history of cooking, dietary anthropology or even old recipe books, -except for notable exceptions such as the recently published Historia de la Gastronomía of our compatriot Néstor Luján-, when even salt and spices were a principal cause for bellicose initiatives and discoveries.

 

            Dietary customs are modulated by, among other factors, tradition, cross-cultural relationships, caloric demands made by the environment and available resources, all constituting very characteristic patterns in each cultural group, which frequently have served as a binding force.

 

            The Canary Islands have been a crossroads of cultures for five centuries. Its indigenous strata is superimposed by the Spanish and Portuguese conquistadores, and to a lesser degree by Jews, Mozarabics, Normans, etc... The importance of the Canary Islands in the navigation routes to America, India and central and south Africa has given the islands an English, Italian, African and Asian influence, as well as an overall Caribbean and Creole character. These influences became even more pronounced with Canarian emigration to America which over time led to the importation of new customs brought back by the Indiano, as were called the Spaniards who returned to their land from America.

 

            The term "traditional", when applied to cooking, is very ambiguous. The dietary customs of the islands must have undergone changes over time and acquired specifically local characteristics because of the difficulty of reaching certain areas, the scarcity of available ingredients and the peculiarity of such adverse environments as Fuerteventura and La Palma.

 

            Throughout history there have been prolonged periods of scarcity, with a very limited variety of food for the common people. On the other hand, we must consider, with respect to the past as well as the present, peculiarities related to social groups or their degree of contact with foreign lands.

 

            What we understand as popular Canarian cooking is a barely uniform conglomeration of dishes in which basic products (gofio, cheese, almonds...) of a subsistence economy are mixed with products characteristic of our ecosystem (parrotfish, palm honey...), foreign dishes that have become popular later (huevos moles, cakes, ribs with corn...) or even recent culinary "inventions" which are treated as traditional (gofio ice cream, avocado with prawns, papaya with orange...), having forgotten many recipes which were indispensable in earlier times and today are unnecessary, as in the case of homemade preserves of meat or fruit. For example, I did not find coriander in any of the 850 recipes that I have used. Generally in Europe only the seeds of this plant are used; however the leaves are frequently used in India from which it seems to have come to be incorporated in Portuguese cooking. One might ask then if our popular "mojo verde" sauce of today (a sauce whose main ingredient is coriander) is nothing more than an adaptation of the sauce that was made traditionally with parsley, after the recent introduction of this plant, possibly by our large hindustani colony.

 

            More recently, as a consequence of the tourist trade, the type of cooking that is offered on the Canary Islands could be considered one of the most international in the world; but this recipe book shows that this culinary wealth is not so recent and that already in the 19th century an international cuisine was being cultivated on the Canary Islands, although on a small scale, and adapted to our environment.

 

            It might even seem surprising that many of these recipes can be found in treatises which today are considered to be classics on the subject of cooking and which were published during the same period or later, such as books by the Countess of Pardo Bazán or the Marquess of Parabere. As proof, coincidences can be found in certain recipes, even in the way they were written, such as the "bollos pardos" of this recipe book and the "mostachones pardos" of the Marquess, the recipe for Queen's cakes -obviously referring to the Queen of England- or French onion soup, which, as is well known, comes from Paris. These examples illustrate that Canarians did not remain isolated from the cultural developments of the time, although the culinary information that was being acquired from abroad was adapted to the possibilities, or perhaps needs, of the local environment.

 

            We see how the recipes overlap so that full advantage can be taken of all the ingredients that make up the recipes. Fruit is eaten in its natural form or in preserves, often using even the peel or the seeds for liqueur. Today's leftover meat is used for tomorrow's stews etc...

 

            Many of the recipes that I include in this book cannot be considered exclusively Canarian. Therefore I would not like it to be interpreted as a treatise on traditional cooking OF the Canary Islands, but rather an example of traditional cooking that has occurred ON the Canary Islands.

 

            This view does not diminish in any way the documentary importance of this book; on the contrary. It is worth knowing our past in order to understand its outstanding aspects within a global culture whose memory must be refreshed periodically. The gastronomic variety, uniqueness and richness of these recipes makes them enormously interesting in themselves, not to mention the fact that I'm sure many of our readers will find in these pages the forgotten pleasures of real home cooking.

 

            Approximately 40% of these recipes were written in the last quarter of the 19th century in Los Silos, a village in the north of the island of Tenerife, by a relative -whose name I do not know- of Miss Constanza Jordán, to whom the notebooks that contain the recipes are dedicated. Another 40% were written in La Laguna, also around the end of the last century, by Delisa Cifra Geraldi, and the remaining 20% in Güimar, in 1863, by Luisa García Díaz.

 

            The illustration on the book's cover shows a portrait of Delisa Cifra and in the family photograph below, taken around the end of the last century, we see at the back Constanza Jordán, already married, standing next to her very tall husband Antonio.

 

            Constanza revealed her cooking secrets to her goddaughter Ursula González Jordán, who, as matriarch of the González family of Guía de Isora, used the recipe books until her death in 1992 at the age of 96.

 

            The recipes written in La Laguna by Delisa Cifra Geraldi were kept first by her daughter Pilar Olivera y Cifra and later by her grandchildren, Pilar, Concepción and María del Carmen de la Rosa Olivera, who continue using them still today.

 

            Finally the recipe book of Luisa García Díaz was given to me by her great grandaugter Ana González Matilla.

 

            I felt that it was necessary to publish the recipes just as they were written originally, however many of them would be difficult to follow today if you had to rely only on the information provided in them. In some cases previous cooking experience, not necessarily so basic, is taken for granted. In other cases references are made to old units of measurement, utensils no longer used or local names of ingredients. Finally, it seemed like it would be prudent to adapt some recipes to current dietary criteria, for example, reducing the content of saturated fats as much as possible.

 

            I have collected more than 850 recipes of which I have omitted duplicates and ones of low nutritional value. Of those remaining I have selected 332, which I have reproduced, trying to follow the criteria and cooking procedures of today at the same time respecting as much as possible the original description. I experimented to check the right quantities of ingredients, cooking times, the sequence of the procedures, and, above all, the final results. I have also included nutritional information for each dish, which I hope will be useful to anyone who wants to be aware of the nutritional value of what they eat.

 

            Finally another group of recipes are included only in their original version, as they are variations of others already described, unique foods that are unreproducible (recipes to make dyes, cultivation and production of indigo, making chocolate from seeds etc...), and canned meat, given the fact that the procedures are not very compatible with today's health standards.  I guarantee the results of my version to anyone who precisely follows the procedures and uses the right quantities, but I accept any criticism that might be made with respect to fidelity to the original recipe. Anyone who does not agree with my interpretation can use the original recipe according to his own judgment. To facilitate the task I have included a small glossary which will help to interpret the meaning of some local or archaic terms.

 

            Good luck and enjoy!

 

 

 

ANA HERNANDEZ
